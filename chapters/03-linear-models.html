3  Linear Models: From Frequentist to Bayesian

You already know linear regression. Or you did, once. You learned \(y = X\beta + \varepsilon\) in a statistics class, ran it in Excel or Python, got a table of coefficients and \(p\)-values, and moved on. That knowledge is the foundation for everything in this chapter.

What we are going to do here is take that familiar model and rebuild it as a Bayesian model in PyMC. You will see that the answers barely change — the posterior means will land right on top of the OLS estimates. But the questions you can ask become richer. Instead of a single best-fit line, you get a distribution over regression lines. Instead of confidence intervals derived from asymptotic theory, you get the full posterior uncertainty over every parameter. And instead of a framework that struggles with outliers, missing data, and small samples, you get one that handles them naturally.

The payoff is not dramatic for simple problems. That is the point. Start where the answers are easy to verify, so you can trust the machinery when the problems get hard.

3.1 OLS Refresher

Linear regression models the relationship between a response variable \(y\) and one or more predictors \(X\) as:

\[ y = X\beta + \varepsilon, \qquad \varepsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2 I) \]

The Ordinary Least Squares (OLS) estimator finds the \(\hat{\beta}\) that minimizes the sum of squared residuals. In matrix form, the closed-form solution is \(\hat{\beta} = (X^\top X)^{-1} X^\top y\). No iteration, no tuning — plug in the data and out comes an answer. That simplicity is what makes OLS the workhorse of applied statistics.

But notice the assumptions baked into that formula. The errors \(\varepsilon\) are normally distributed, have constant variance (homoscedasticity), and are independent of each other. When these assumptions hold, OLS is hard to beat. When they break down — outliers, heteroscedasticity, correlated errors, small samples — the guarantees evaporate. Keep that in mind as we move through the chapter.

Statsmodels gives you OLS in a few lines. Let us generate some data we can use throughout the chapter. We will simulate a simple relationship: house price (in thousands of dollars) as a function of square footage, with Gaussian noise.

Take a moment to notice what OLS gives you: a single best-fit line, a table of standard errors derived from the residual variance, and confidence intervals computed from the \(t\)-distribution. These are all functions of the data and the model assumptions. If the assumptions hold, they are exactly right. If they do not, you have no easy way to know how far off they are. The Bayesian approach will give us a different way to think about these same quantities.

3.2 The Bayesian Linear Model

The Bayesian version of linear regression uses the same likelihood:

\[ y_i \sim \mathcal{N}(\beta_0 + \beta_1 x_i,\; \sigma^2) \]

The difference is that we place prior distributions on the unknown parameters \(\beta_0\), \(\beta_1\), and \(\sigma\). Instead of computing a single point estimate, Bayes’ theorem gives us a posterior distribution over all three parameters simultaneously:

\[ p(\beta_0, \beta_1, \sigma \mid y, X) \propto p(y \mid X, \beta_0, \beta_1, \sigma) \; p(\beta_0) \; p(\beta_1) \; p(\sigma) \]

Think of it this way. OLS draws one line through the data — the line that minimizes squared error. The Bayesian model draws many lines, each weighted by how well it explains the data and how plausible it is under the priors. The posterior is the full collection of plausible regression lines, not just the single best one.

For weakly informative priors — priors that rule out absurd values but do not strongly favor any particular answer — the posterior will be nearly identical to the OLS result. That is not a bug. It is a sanity check. The priors are doing their job: letting the data speak while gently constraining the model to reasonable territory.

Here is the generative model as a plate diagram:

\[ \boxed{ \begin{aligned} \beta_0 &\sim \mathcal{N}(0, 200) \\ \beta_1 &\sim \mathcal{N}(0, 10) \\ \sigma &\sim \text{HalfNormal}(50) \\ \mu_i &= \beta_0 + \beta_1 \, x_i \\ y_i &\sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_i, \sigma) \quad \text{for } i = 1, \ldots, n \end{aligned} } \]

Each prior says: “I do not know the exact value, but I can rule out values that are physically nonsensical.” The normal priors on \(\beta_0\) and \(\beta_1\) are centered at zero with wide standard deviations. The half-normal prior on \(\sigma\) forces the noise scale to be positive. These are not strong opinions — they are guardrails.

A common worry at this stage is: “Am I not biasing the results by choosing priors?” With weakly informative priors like these, the answer is barely. The prior \(\mathcal{N}(0, 200)\) on the intercept says the intercept is somewhere between roughly \(-400\) and \(400\) — hardly a controversial claim for house prices in the hundreds of thousands. The data will overwhelm these priors. If you picked a prior of \(\mathcal{N}(0, 0.01)\), that would bias the results. The distinction between “weakly informative” and “informative” priors is one of the most important practical skills in Bayesian modeling, and we will explore it directly in Exercise 1.

3.3 Building It in PyMC

Now we translate that generative model into PyMC code. Every line maps directly to a line in the math above.

import pymc as pm
import arviz as az

with pm.Model() as linear_model:
    # Priors
    beta_0 = pm.Normal("beta_0", mu=0, sigma=200)
    beta_1 = pm.Normal("beta_1", mu=0, sigma=10)
    sigma = pm.HalfNormal("sigma", sigma=50)

    # Expected value
    mu = beta_0 + beta_1 * df["sqft"].values

    # Likelihood
    y_obs = pm.Normal(
        "y_obs",
        mu=mu,
        sigma=sigma,
        observed=df["price"].values,
    )

    # Sample from the posterior
    trace = pm.sample(
        draws=2000,
        tune=1000,
        chains=4,
        random_seed=42,
        progressbar=True,
    )






Let us walk through each piece:


    

  • pm.Normal("beta_0", mu=0, sigma=200) creates a random variable named beta_0 with a normal prior. The wide standard deviation of 200 means we are not strongly opinionated about the intercept.
    • 

  • pm.HalfNormal("sigma", sigma=50) creates a positive-valued random variable for the noise scale. HalfNormal is a normal distribution folded at zero — it only takes positive values, which is exactly what a standard deviation must be.
    • 

  • mu = beta_0 + beta_1 * df["sqft"].values is a deterministic transformation. PyMC tracks this computation through its graph, so the sampler knows how \(\mu\) depends on \(\beta_0\) and \(\beta_1\).
    • 

  • pm.Normal("y_obs", ..., observed=df["price"].values) is the likelihood. The observed keyword pins this distribution to the actual data. This is what connects the model to reality.
    • 

  • pm.sample() runs the MCMC sampler (NUTS, by default) to draw samples from the posterior distribution. We draw 2000 samples per chain across 4 chains, giving us 8000 posterior draws in total.
    •

A quick sanity check for any Bayesian model







Chris Fonnesbeck offers a simple diagnostic: “The posterior should sit between the prior and the likelihood — if it does not, check your model.” For our linear model, the posterior means land between the prior means (zero, from our weakly informative priors) and the maximum likelihood estimates (the OLS values). Because the priors are wide and the data are plentiful, the posterior is pulled almost entirely toward the data. If the posterior were outside the range spanned by the prior and the likelihood — for instance, farther from zero than the OLS estimate despite a prior centred at zero — that would signal a coding error or a misspecified model. Get in the habit of checking this before interpreting results.






But the Bayesian version gives you something OLS does not (at least not as naturally): the full posterior distribution, not just point estimates. Let us compare the confidence intervals from OLS with the Highest Density Intervals (HDIs) from the posterior:

3.5 Why Bother Being Bayesian for Linear Regression?


If the answers are the same, why go through the trouble of priors and MCMC? Because the answers are the same only for simple, clean problems. The Bayesian framework pays off when things get messy:


1. Regularization via priors. Setting a tighter prior on \(\beta\) is mathematically equivalent to Ridge regression. A \(\mathcal{N}(0,
\tau^2)\) prior on the coefficients adds an \(L_2\) penalty of strength \(\sigma^2 / \tau^2\). But unlike Ridge, you do not need to tune a regularization hyperparameter — the data determine how much the prior should be trusted.


2. Full uncertainty propagation. When you predict a new observation, the Bayesian model propagates uncertainty through the entire chain: uncertainty in \(\beta_0\), in \(\beta_1\), in \(\sigma\), and the observation noise. OLS prediction intervals approximate this, but the Bayesian version handles it exactly even for small samples and nonlinear transformations.


3. Natural handling of small samples. With 10 data points, OLS confidence intervals rely on asymptotic approximations that may not hold. The Bayesian posterior is exact (given the model). Informative priors can stabilize estimates when data are scarce.


4. Easy extension to hierarchical models. Once you have a Bayesian linear model, adding group structure (different slopes for different cities, different intercepts for different products) is a matter of adding one more level of priors. We will see this in ?sec-hierarchical.


5. Robust alternatives. Replacing the Normal likelihood with a Student-\(t\) makes the model robust to outliers. In OLS, you would need to detect and remove outliers manually. In the Bayesian version, you swap one line of code.


The point is not that Bayesian linear regression is better than OLS for textbook problems. It is that the Bayesian framework is a superset: everything OLS can do, Bayesian regression can do too — and it extends gracefully to problems where OLS breaks down.

3.6.1 Why centering and scaling helps MCMC


Before fitting, center and scale the predictors. This is not just good practice — it materially helps the MCMC sampler. When predictors are on different scales (square footage in the thousands, bedrooms in single digits), the posterior geometry becomes an elongated, tilted ellipse. The sampler has to take tiny steps to navigate it, leading to slow mixing and high autocorrelation.


Standardizing the predictors makes the posterior more spherical, which lets the sampler take bigger steps and explore efficiently.




# Standardize predictors
sqft_mean, sqft_sd = df_multi["sqft"].mean(), df_multi["sqft"].std()
bed_mean, bed_sd = df_multi["bedrooms"].mean(), df_multi["bedrooms"].std()

sqft_z = (df_multi["sqft"] - sqft_mean) / sqft_sd
bed_z = (df_multi["bedrooms"] - bed_mean) / bed_sd






with pm.Model() as multi_model:

3.7 Bayesian Logistic Regression


Not all outcomes are continuous. When the response is binary — yes/no, click/no-click, survived/died — we need logistic regression. If you have used scikit-learn’s LogisticRegression, you have already fit this model. The idea is the same as linear regression: a linear combination of predictors determines the outcome. But the outcome is a probability bounded between 0 and 1, so we pass the linear combination through the logistic (sigmoid) function:


\[
p_i = \text{logit}^{-1}(\beta_0 + \beta_1 x_i) = \frac{1}{1 + e^{-(\beta_0 + \beta_1 x_i)}}
\]


\[
y_i \sim \text{Bernoulli}(p_i)
\]


Let us simulate a dataset where the probability of passing an exam depends on hours of study:




# Simulate logistic data: P(pass) depends on hours studied
n_students = 120
hours = rng.uniform(0, 10, size=n_students)
true_b0_log = -3.0
true_b1_log = 0.8
logit_p = true_b0_log + true_b1_log * hours
prob_pass = 1.0 / (1.0 + np.exp(-logit_p))
passed = rng.binomial(n=1, p=prob_pass)

print(f"Pass rate: {passed.mean():.2%}")

This spaghetti plot is one of the most useful visualizations in Bayesian analysis. Every gray line represents one plausible logistic curve drawn from the posterior. A tight bundle of lines means the model is confident; a wide fan means it is not. For a decision-maker — say, a university administrator deciding how many study hours to recommend — the spread of those curves is just as important as their center. The frequentist approach gives you a single curve and a confidence interval table. The Bayesian approach gives you a picture you can look at and immediately understand.






3.8 Robust Regression


Real-world data have outliers. OLS treats every residual equally, so a single extreme point can drag the entire regression line. The classic frequentist fix is to detect outliers (Cook’s distance, studentized residuals) and remove them. The Bayesian fix is more elegant: replace the Normal likelihood with a Student-\(t\) distribution.


The Student-\(t\) has heavier tails than the Normal. That means it assigns more probability to extreme values, so outliers are “expected” by the model rather than treated as anomalies. The key parameter is the degrees of freedom \(\nu\): small \(\nu\) means heavy tails (robust); as \(\nu \to \infty\), the \(t\) converges to the Normal.


Let us see this in action with contaminated data:

This is worth pausing on, because it illustrates a broader principle. In classical statistics, robustness is achieved by detecting and removing problematic observations — a two-step process that requires judgment calls about which points to exclude. In Bayesian modeling, robustness is achieved by choosing a better model — one that accounts for the possibility of extreme values from the start. The outliers are not removed; they are simply given less influence because the model expects them. This is a fundamentally different philosophy: instead of cleaning data to fit the model, you adjust the model to fit the data.












Why heavy tails matter: a lesson from finance







Thomas Wiecki puts it bluntly: “The Normal distribution assumes 2008-scale crashes never happen; the Student-\(t\) is more honest.” Under a Gaussian likelihood, a six-sigma event has a probability on the order of \(10^{-9}\). In financial data, such events happen every few years. The Normal model is not just slightly wrong — it is systematically blind to the most consequential observations.


The practical advice is simple: whenever your data could plausibly contain extreme values — financial returns, insurance claims, network traffic, sensor readings in harsh environments — default to a heavy-tailed likelihood. The Student-\(t\) with a learned \(\nu\) is the easiest upgrade: if the data are well behaved, \(\nu\) will be estimated large and the model reduces to a Normal. If the data have genuine heavy tails, \(\nu\) shrinks and the model accommodates them. You pay almost nothing for this insurance.

3.9 Prediction with Uncertainty


One of the most practical things you can do with a Bayesian model is make predictions that carry uncertainty. Not “the predicted price is $350k” but “the predicted price is $350k, with a 95% chance of being between $290k and $410k.”


PyMC provides pm.sample_posterior_predictive() for exactly this. It takes each posterior draw of the parameters, plugs them into the likelihood, and generates a simulated \(y\) value. The result is the posterior predictive distribution: the full distribution of outcomes you might see for new data, given everything the model has learned.

## OLS Refresher

Linear regression models the relationship between a response variable
$y$ and one or more predictors $X$ as:

$$
y = X\beta + \varepsilon, \qquad \varepsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2 I)
$$

The Ordinary Least Squares (OLS) estimator finds the $\hat{\beta}$ that
minimizes the sum of squared residuals. In matrix form, the closed-form
solution is $\hat{\beta} = (X^\top X)^{-1} X^\top y$. No iteration, no
tuning --- plug in the data and out comes an answer. That simplicity is
what makes OLS the workhorse of applied statistics.

But notice the assumptions baked into that formula. The errors
$\varepsilon$ are normally distributed, have constant variance
(homoscedasticity), and are independent of each other. When these
assumptions hold, OLS is hard to beat. When they break down --- outliers,
heteroscedasticity, correlated errors, small samples --- the guarantees
evaporate. Keep that in mind as we move through the chapter.

Statsmodels gives you OLS in a few lines. Let us generate some data
we can use throughout the chapter. We will simulate a simple
relationship: house price (in thousands of dollars) as a function of
square footage, with Gaussian noise.

## The Bayesian Linear Model

The Bayesian version of linear regression uses the same likelihood:

$$
y_i \sim \mathcal{N}(\beta_0 + \beta_1 x_i,\; \sigma^2)
$$

The difference is that we place **prior distributions** on the unknown
parameters $\beta_0$, $\beta_1$, and $\sigma$. Instead of computing a
single point estimate, Bayes' theorem gives us a **posterior
distribution** over all three parameters simultaneously:

$$
p(\beta_0, \beta_1, \sigma \mid y, X) \propto
  p(y \mid X, \beta_0, \beta_1, \sigma) \;
  p(\beta_0) \; p(\beta_1) \; p(\sigma)
$$

Think of it this way. OLS draws *one* line through the data --- the line
that minimizes squared error. The Bayesian model draws *many* lines,
each weighted by how well it explains the data and how plausible it is
under the priors. The posterior is the full collection of plausible
regression lines, not just the single best one.

For weakly informative priors --- priors that rule out absurd values but
do not strongly favor any particular answer --- the posterior will be
nearly identical to the OLS result. That is not a bug. It is a sanity
check. The priors are doing their job: letting the data speak while
gently constraining the model to reasonable territory.

Here is the generative model as a plate diagram:

$$
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
\beta_0 &\sim \mathcal{N}(0, 200) \\
\beta_1 &\sim \mathcal{N}(0, 10) \\
\sigma &\sim \text{HalfNormal}(50) \\
\mu_i &= \beta_0 + \beta_1 \, x_i \\
y_i &\sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_i, \sigma) \quad \text{for } i = 1, \ldots, n
\end{aligned}
}
$$

Each prior says: "I do not know the exact value, but I can rule out
values that are physically nonsensical." The normal priors on
$\beta_0$ and $\beta_1$ are centered at zero with wide standard
deviations. The half-normal prior on $\sigma$ forces the noise scale
to be positive. These are not strong opinions --- they are guardrails.

A common worry at this stage is: "Am I not biasing the results by
choosing priors?" With weakly informative priors like these, the answer
is *barely*. The prior $\mathcal{N}(0, 200)$ on the intercept says the
intercept is somewhere between roughly $-400$ and $400$ --- hardly a
controversial claim for house prices in the hundreds of thousands. The
data will overwhelm these priors. If you picked a prior of
$\mathcal{N}(0, 0.01)$, *that* would bias the results. The distinction
between "weakly informative" and "informative" priors is one of the most
important practical skills in Bayesian modeling, and we will explore it
directly in Exercise 1.


## Building It in PyMC

Now we translate that generative model into PyMC code. Every line
maps directly to a line in the math above.

```{python}
#| label: pymc-model
import pymc as pm
import arviz as az

with pm.Model() as linear_model:
    # Priors
    beta_0 = pm.Normal("beta_0", mu=0, sigma=200)
    beta_1 = pm.Normal("beta_1", mu=0, sigma=10)
    sigma = pm.HalfNormal("sigma", sigma=50)

    # Expected value
    mu = beta_0 + beta_1 * df["sqft"].values

    # Likelihood
    y_obs = pm.Normal(
        "y_obs",
        mu=mu,
        sigma=sigma,
        observed=df["price"].values,
    )

    # Sample from the posterior
    trace = pm.sample(
        draws=2000,
        tune=1000,
        chains=4,
        random_seed=42,
        progressbar=True,
    )
```

Let us walk through each piece:

- `pm.Normal("beta_0", mu=0, sigma=200)` creates a random variable
  named `beta_0` with a normal prior. The wide standard deviation of
  200 means we are not strongly opinionated about the intercept.

- `pm.HalfNormal("sigma", sigma=50)` creates a positive-valued random
  variable for the noise scale. `HalfNormal` is a normal distribution
  folded at zero --- it only takes positive values, which is exactly
  what a standard deviation must be.

- `mu = beta_0 + beta_1 * df["sqft"].values` is a deterministic
  transformation. PyMC tracks this computation through its graph, so
  the sampler knows how $\mu$ depends on $\beta_0$ and $\beta_1$.

- `pm.Normal("y_obs", ..., observed=df["price"].values)` is the
  likelihood. The `observed` keyword pins this distribution to the
  actual data. This is what connects the model to reality.

- `pm.sample()` runs the MCMC sampler (NUTS, by default) to draw
  samples from the posterior distribution. We draw 2000 samples per
  chain across 4 chains, giving us 8000 posterior draws in total.

Now check the diagnostics:


## A quick sanity check for any Bayesian model

Chris Fonnesbeck offers a simple diagnostic: "The posterior
should sit between the prior and the likelihood --- if it does
not, check your model." For our linear model, the posterior
means land between the prior means (zero, from our weakly
informative priors) and the maximum likelihood estimates (the
OLS values). Because the priors are wide and the data are
plentiful, the posterior is pulled almost entirely toward the
data. If the posterior were *outside* the range spanned by the
prior and the likelihood --- for instance, farther from zero
than the OLS estimate despite a prior centred at zero --- that
would signal a coding error or a misspecified model. Get in the
habit of checking this before interpreting results.

## Why Bother Being Bayesian for Linear Regression?

If the answers are the same, why go through the trouble of priors and
MCMC? Because the answers are the same *only* for simple, clean
problems. The Bayesian framework pays off when things get messy:

**1. Regularization via priors.** Setting a tighter prior on $\beta$ is
mathematically equivalent to Ridge regression. A $\mathcal{N}(0,
\tau^2)$ prior on the coefficients adds an $L_2$ penalty of strength
$\sigma^2 / \tau^2$. But unlike Ridge, you do not need to tune a
regularization hyperparameter --- the data determine how much the prior
should be trusted.

**2. Full uncertainty propagation.** When you predict a new observation,
the Bayesian model propagates uncertainty through the entire chain:
uncertainty in $\beta_0$, in $\beta_1$, in $\sigma$, and the
observation noise. OLS prediction intervals approximate this, but the
Bayesian version handles it exactly even for small samples and
nonlinear transformations.

**3. Natural handling of small samples.** With 10 data points, OLS
confidence intervals rely on asymptotic approximations that may not
hold. The Bayesian posterior is exact (given the model). Informative
priors can stabilize estimates when data are scarce.

**4. Easy extension to hierarchical models.** Once you have a Bayesian
linear model, adding group structure (different slopes for different
cities, different intercepts for different products) is a matter of
adding one more level of priors. We will see this in @sec-hierarchical.

**5. Robust alternatives.** Replacing the Normal likelihood with a
Student-$t$ makes the model robust to outliers. In OLS, you would need
to detect and remove outliers manually. In the Bayesian version, you
swap one line of code.

The point is not that Bayesian linear regression is better than OLS for
textbook problems. It is that the Bayesian framework is a *superset*:
everything OLS can do, Bayesian regression can do too --- and it extends
gracefully to problems where OLS breaks down.

### Why centering and scaling helps MCMC

Before fitting, center and scale the predictors. This is not just good
practice --- it materially helps the MCMC sampler. When predictors are on
different scales (square footage in the thousands, bedrooms in single
digits), the posterior geometry becomes an elongated, tilted ellipse.
The sampler has to take tiny steps to navigate it, leading to slow
mixing and high autocorrelation.

Standardizing the predictors makes the posterior more spherical, which
lets the sampler take bigger steps and explore efficiently.

```{python}
#| label: multi-center
# Standardize predictors
sqft_mean, sqft_sd = df_multi["sqft"].mean(), df_multi["sqft"].std()
bed_mean, bed_sd = df_multi["bedrooms"].mean(), df_multi["bedrooms"].std()

sqft_z = (df_multi["sqft"] - sqft_mean) / sqft_sd
bed_z = (df_multi["bedrooms"] - bed_mean) / bed_sd
```

```{python}
#| label: multi-model
with pm.Model() as multi_model:
    # Priors on standardized scale
    beta_0 = pm.Normal("beta_0", mu=0, sigma=200)
    beta_sqft = pm.Normal("beta_sqft", mu=0, sigma=50)
    beta_bed = pm.Normal("beta_bed", mu=0, sigma=50)
    sigma = pm.HalfNormal("sigma", sigma=50)

    mu = beta_0 + beta_sqft * sqft_z.values + beta_bed * bed_z.values

    y_obs = pm.Normal(
        "y_obs",
        mu=mu,
        sigma=sigma,
        observed=df_multi["price"].values,
    )

    trace_multi = pm.sample(
        draws=2000,
        tune=1000,
        chains=4,
        random_seed=42,
    )
```

```{python}
#| label: multi-summary
az.summary(
    trace_multi,
    var_names=["beta_0", "beta_sqft", "beta_bed", "sigma"],
)
```

Because the predictors are standardized, the coefficient magnitudes
are directly comparable: a one-standard-deviation increase in square
footage vs. a one-standard-deviation increase in bedrooms. To recover
the coefficients on the original scale, back-transform:
$\beta_{\text{orig}} = \beta_{\text{std}} / \text{sd}(x)$.

```{python}

slope_bed_orig = post["beta_bed"] / bed_sd

      f"{float(slope_sqft_orig.mean()):.4f}")
print(f"Slope (bedrooms), original scale: "
      f"{float(slope_bed_orig.mean()):.2f}")
print(f"True slope (sqft):                {true_slope}")
print(f"True slope (bedrooms):            {true_beta_bed}")
```

The pattern for adding more predictors is the same: add a prior, add a
term to $\mu$. The model scales linearly with the number of features.


## Bayesian Logistic Regression

Not all outcomes are continuous. When the response is binary --- yes/no,
click/no-click, survived/died --- we need logistic regression. If you have
used scikit-learn's `LogisticRegression`, you have already fit this
model. The idea is the same as linear regression: a linear combination
of predictors determines the outcome. But the outcome is a probability
bounded between 0 and 1, so we pass the linear combination through the
**logistic (sigmoid) function**:

$$
p_i = \text{logit}^{-1}(\beta_0 + \beta_1 x_i) = \frac{1}{1 + e^{-(\beta_0 + \beta_1 x_i)}}
$$

$$
y_i \sim \text{Bernoulli}(p_i)
$$

Let us simulate a dataset where the probability of passing an exam
depends on hours of study:

```{python}
#| label: logistic-data
# Simulate logistic data: P(pass) depends on hours studied
n_students = 120
hours = rng.uniform(0, 10, size=n_students)
true_b0_log = -3.0
true_b1_log = 0.8
logit_p = true_b0_log + true_b1_log * hours
prob_pass = 1.0 / (1.0 + np.exp(-logit_p))
passed = rng.binomial(n=1, p=prob_pass)

print(f"Pass rate: {passed.mean():.2%}")
```

```{python}
#| label: logistic-model
with pm.Model() as logistic_model:
    b0 = pm.Normal("b0", mu=0, sigma=5)
    b1 = pm.Normal("b1", mu=0, sigma=5)

    # Linear predictor passed through sigmoid
    p = pm.math.sigmoid(b0 + b1 * hours)

    # Bernoulli likelihood
    y_obs = pm.Bernoulli("y_obs", p=p, observed=passed)

    trace_logistic = pm.sample(
        draws=2000,
        tune=1000,
        chains=4,
        random_seed=42,
    )
```

This spaghetti plot is one of the most useful visualizations in Bayesian
analysis. Every gray line represents one plausible logistic curve drawn
from the posterior. A tight bundle of lines means the model is confident;
a wide fan means it is not. For a decision-maker --- say, a university
administrator deciding how many study hours to recommend --- the spread
of those curves is just as important as their center. The frequentist
approach gives you a single curve and a confidence interval table. The
Bayesian approach gives you a picture you can *look at* and immediately
understand.


## Robust Regression

Real-world data have outliers. OLS treats every residual equally, so a
single extreme point can drag the entire regression line. The classic
frequentist fix is to detect outliers (Cook's distance, studentized
residuals) and remove them. The Bayesian fix is more elegant: replace
the Normal likelihood with a **Student-$t$ distribution**.

The Student-$t$ has heavier tails than the Normal. That means it
assigns more probability to extreme values, so outliers are "expected"
by the model rather than treated as anomalies. The key parameter is the
degrees of freedom $\nu$: small $\nu$ means heavy tails (robust); as
$\nu \to \infty$, the $t$ converges to the Normal.

Let us see this in action with contaminated data:

```{python}
#| label: robust-data
# Generate clean data plus 5% contamination
n_robust = 100
x_robust = rng.uniform(0, 10, size=n_robust)
y_clean = 10 + 3 * x_robust + rng.normal(0, 3, size=n_robust)

# Add 5% outliers
n_outliers = int(0.05 * n_robust)
outlier_idx = rng.choice(n_robust, size=n_outliers, replace=False)
y_contaminated = y_clean.copy()
y_contaminated[outlier_idx] += rng.uniform(30, 60, size=n_outliers)

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(8, 5))
ax.scatter(
    x_robust,
    y_contaminated,
    alpha=0.6,
    s=30,
    color="steelblue",
)
ax.scatter(
    x_robust[outlier_idx],
    y_contaminated[outlier_idx],
    color="red",
    s=60,
    zorder=5,
    label=f"Outliers ({n_outliers} points)",
    edgecolor="black",
    linewidth=0.5,
)
ax.set_xlabel("x")
ax.set_ylabel("y")
ax.set_title("Data with 5% contamination")
ax.legend()
plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()
```

Now fit two models --- one with a Normal likelihood, one with a
Student-$t$ likelihood --- and compare:

```{python}
#| label: robust-models
# Model 1: Normal likelihood (sensitive to outliers)
with pm.Model() as normal_model:
    b0 = pm.Normal("b0", mu=0, sigma=50)
    b1 = pm.Normal("b1", mu=0, sigma=20)
    sigma = pm.HalfNormal("sigma", sigma=20)
    mu = b0 + b1 * x_robust
    y_obs = pm.Normal("y_obs", mu=mu, sigma=sigma, observed=y_contaminated)
    trace_normal = pm.sample(
        draws=2000,
        tune=1000,
        chains=4,
        random_seed=42,
    )

# Model 2: Student-t likelihood (robust to outliers)
with pm.Model() as robust_model:
    b0 = pm.Normal("b0", mu=0, sigma=50)
    b1 = pm.Normal("b1", mu=0, sigma=20)
    sigma = pm.HalfNormal("sigma", sigma=20)
    nu = pm.Exponential("nu", lam=1 / 30)  # prior on degrees of freedom
    mu = b0 + b1 * x_robust
    y_obs = pm.StudentT(
        "y_obs",
        nu=nu,
        mu=mu,
        sigma=sigma,
        observed=y_contaminated,
    )
    trace_robust = pm.sample(
        draws=2000,
        tune=1000,
        chains=4,
        random_seed=42,
    )
```

slope and intercept are biased. The Student-$t$ model (orange) shrugs
off the contamination and recovers a line much closer to the truth
(black dashed). The only change was swapping `pm.Normal` for
`pm.StudentT` and adding a prior on $\nu$. One line of code buys you
robustness that would take pages of diagnostics in the frequentist
approach.

This is worth pausing on, because it illustrates a broader principle.
In classical statistics, robustness is achieved by *detecting and
removing* problematic observations --- a two-step process that requires
judgment calls about which points to exclude. In Bayesian modeling,
robustness is achieved by *choosing a better model* --- one that
accounts for the possibility of extreme values from the start. The
outliers are not removed; they are simply given less influence because
the model expects them. This is a fundamentally different philosophy:
instead of cleaning data to fit the model, you adjust the model to fit
the data.

::: {.callout-note}
## Why heavy tails matter: a lesson from finance

Thomas Wiecki puts it bluntly: "The Normal distribution assumes
2008-scale crashes never happen; the Student-$t$ is more honest."
Under a Gaussian likelihood, a six-sigma event has a probability
on the order of $10^{-9}$. In financial data, such events happen
every few years. The Normal model is not just slightly wrong ---
it is *systematically* blind to the most consequential
observations.

The practical advice is simple: whenever your data could
plausibly contain extreme values --- financial returns, insurance
claims, network traffic, sensor readings in harsh environments
--- default to a heavy-tailed likelihood. The Student-$t$ with a
learned $\nu$ is the easiest upgrade: if the data are well
behaved, $\nu$ will be estimated large and the model reduces to
a Normal. If the data have genuine heavy tails, $\nu$ shrinks
and the model accommodates them. You pay almost nothing for this
insurance.
:::

```{python}
nu_samples = trace_robust.posterior["nu"].values.flatten()
print(f"Posterior mean of nu: {nu_samples.mean():.1f}")
print(f"94% HDI of nu: [{np.percentile(nu_samples, 3):.1f}, "
      f"{np.percentile(nu_samples, 97):.1f}]")
```

A small $\nu$ (say, below 10) tells you the model found evidence of
heavy tails --- meaning the outliers are real, not just quirks of the
Normal assumption. A large $\nu$ (above 30) means the data are
well-behaved and the $t$ is essentially acting like a Normal.


## Prediction with Uncertainty

One of the most practical things you can do with a Bayesian model is
make predictions that carry uncertainty. Not "the predicted price is
\$350k" but "the predicted price is \$350k, with a 95% chance of being
between \$290k and \$410k."

PyMC provides `pm.sample_posterior_predictive()` for exactly this. It
takes each posterior draw of the parameters, plugs them into the
likelihood, and generates a simulated $y$ value. The result is the
**posterior predictive distribution**: the full distribution of outcomes
you might see for new data, given everything the model has learned.

```{python}
#| label: prediction
# Predict on a grid of new sqft values using the simple model
sqft_new = np.linspace(600, 3200, 100)

# Use the already-fitted trace to generate predictions
# by computing mu and adding noise from the posterior
beta_0_s = trace.posterior["beta_0"].values.flatten()
beta_1_s = trace.posterior["beta_1"].values.flatten()
sigma_s = trace.posterior["sigma"].values.flatten()

# For each posterior sample, compute predicted y at new sqft
y_pred_all = np.zeros((len(beta_0_s), len(sqft_new)))
for i in range(len(beta_0_s)):
    mu_i = beta_0_s[i] + beta_1_s[i] * sqft_new
    y_pred_all[i] = rng.normal(mu_i, sigma_s[i])
```

```{python}
#| label: fig-prediction
#| fig-cap: "Posterior predictive distribution: the shaded band shows the 95% prediction interval, accounting for both parameter uncertainty and observation noise."
y_pred = y_pred_all

pred_mean = y_pred.mean(axis=0)
pred_lower = np.percentile(y_pred, 2.5, axis=0)
pred_upper = np.percentile(y_pred, 97.5, axis=0)
pred_10 = np.percentile(y_pred, 10, axis=0)
pred_90 = np.percentile(y_pred, 90, axis=0)

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(8, 5))
ax.scatter(
    df["sqft"],
    df["price"],
    alpha=0.5,
    s=30,
    color="steelblue",
    label="Observed data",
)
ax.plot(
    sqft_new,
    pred_mean,
    color="black",
    linewidth=2,
    label="Posterior mean",
)
ax.fill_between(
    sqft_new,
    pred_lower,
    pred_upper,
    alpha=0.15,
    color="orange",
    label="95% prediction interval",
)
ax.fill_between(
    sqft_new,
    pred_10,
    pred_90,
    alpha=0.25,
    color="orange",
    label="80% prediction interval",
)
ax.set_xlabel("Square footage")
ax.set_ylabel("Price ($k)")
ax.set_title("Bayesian Predictions with Uncertainty")
ax.legend(loc="upper left")
plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()
```

Notice two sources of uncertainty in the prediction band:

1. **Parameter uncertainty** --- we are not sure exactly where the
   regression line sits (this is the uncertainty in $\beta_0$ and
   $\beta_1$).

2. **Observation noise** --- even if we knew the line perfectly, new
   observations scatter around it according to $\sigma$.

The posterior predictive distribution captures *both*. OLS prediction
intervals do something similar, but they rely on normality assumptions
and large-sample approximations. The Bayesian version works exactly the
same way whether you have 10 observations or 10,000.

In practice, posterior predictive checks are also a powerful model
diagnostic. If your prediction intervals are too narrow (real data
falls outside them too often), the model is overconfident --- maybe you
need heavier tails or more predictors. If the intervals are too wide,
the model is not learning enough from the data --- maybe your priors are
too strong or you are missing a key feature. We will return to this
idea in @sec-bayesian-workflow.

Refit the simple linear model from @sec-building-it-in-pymc with a

```{python}
    y_obs = pm.Normal(
        "y_obs",
        mu=mu,
        sigma=sigma,
        observed=df["price"].values,
    )
    trace_tight = pm.sample(
        draws=2000,
        tune=1000,
        chains=4,
        random_seed=42,

```

The tight prior pulls $\beta_1$ toward zero. With $\sigma = 0.01$, the

prior overwhelms the evidence. The lesson: wide priors let the data
speak; narrow priors encode strong beliefs that had better be right.

## Summary

This chapter covered a lot of ground, but the core message is simple.
The Bayesian linear model is the same linear model you already know,
dressed up with priors and solved with MCMC instead of algebra. For
clean data with enough observations, the Bayesian and frequentist
answers agree. The Bayesian version earns its keep when you need
robustness (Student-$t$), uncertainty propagation (posterior predictive),
regularization (informative priors), or extensibility (hierarchical
models in the next chapter).

The pattern you learned here --- define priors, write the likelihood,
sample, check diagnostics, interpret --- is the same pattern for every
PyMC model in this book. The models get more complex; the workflow does
not.
